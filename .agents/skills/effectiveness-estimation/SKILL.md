# Effectiveness Estimation

## Our goal with these estimates

We want these estimates to be the best all-things-considered attempt at estimating effectiveness of cause areas and recipients in the world. Do extremely deep research, reason very hard and very carefully, and try to produce very clear and well-justified estimates that are also easy for smart lay-people to read. Research depth and published length are different things: research exhaustively, then publish only enough of that work to make the estimate understandable, auditable, and not misleading.

These estimates are based on the premise that we can in theory convert any benefit to some equivalent amount of QALYs. Similar to how any commodity has a conversion into money. There is SOME amount of QALYs that someone would be indifferent to receiving vs. some benefit under consideration.
Therefore we should not give caveats about how QALYs are a "narrow" metric, and we shouldn't feel compelled to focus on easily measurable health effects of interventions just because QALY is our unit of measurement. We want to capture the main significant effects of interventions no matter what form they take, and express them in terms of QALYs even if this involves approximation and uncertainty.

## Core Principles

When working on effectiveness estimations, always:

1. **Make sure assumptions are factored out explicitly and listed in the assumptions section** — For any number that is introduced in a way where it affects the calculation of one of the point estimates, it should be listed in the assumptions. The goal is extreme transparency.
2. **Write so a smart lay reader can hold the reasoning in their head** — Clarity and concision are one goal, not two. After the research and calculations are done, make a deliberate compression pass before publishing. A non-expert who reads the page once should come away able to state: what the estimate is, the simple calculation or comparison that drives it, the few assumptions that matter most, why the plausible range is wide or narrow, and what would most move the estimate up or down.
   - **Make each sentence earn its place.** Keep a sentence only if it states a load-bearing number or claim, justifies a contestable choice, defines a term or anchor the reader needs, flags a major uncertainty, or prevents a likely misunderstanding. Cut sentences that restate, narrate your process ("we considered X but…"), or re-hedge an already-hedged point. Sentences need not be short — they must be load-bearing; when unsure, cut. For example, replace "This estimate, while grounded in a reasonable reading of the evidence, should be understood as carrying substantial uncertainty; our central figure, about $5,000 per life, is best read not as a precise forecast but as a rough indication sensitive to several contestable assumptions discussed below." with "We estimate about $5,000 per life — an order of magnitude, not a forecast. It rests on a few contestable assumptions (below)."
   - **Lead with the shape.** The reader should be able to grasp the overall structure of the argument — what the estimate is and what drives it — before wading into details, rather than reconstructing it from scattered pieces. How you achieve that is a judgment call, not a fixed template.
   - **Push deep detail into collapsible blocks.** When a parameter derivation, sensitivity sweep, or worked calculation supports a claim but would bury the line of argument, wrap it in a `:::details{title="…"}` block (syntax in `content/CLAUDE.md`) so it renders collapsed by default — the claim and its result stay in the always-visible prose, and the expand holds the derivation. Never hide load-bearing content this way: if the reader needs it to follow the argument, it stays visible. The visible lead must carry standalone substance — a claim, result, or number the reader can use without expanding; if collapsing would leave only a pointer ("details below"), surface the key result or don't use a collapsible at all. When the detail is short and trivial, just state it compactly inline rather than wrapping it.
   - **Hedge once.** State a caveat in its single best home; don't re-qualify the same number every time it reappears. Send discarded alternatives, source-hunting notes, and editor-facing nuance to `# Internal Notes`, not the published prose.
   - **Check it cold.** Before finishing, reread the page as a smart non-expert: can you state its logic in a few sentences, and does skimming the structure give the gist? If not, the shape is buried — fix the structure, not the wording.
3. **Don't make references to what previous version of the estimates claimed** - We can't expect readers to track the edit history of these documents. Only refer to reasoning that exists within the current documents. The goal is for these justifications to stand on their own.
4. **Don't be too anchored to the existing estimates** - If your research and reasoning leads you to think that some estimate should be different, change it. Don't simply try to create a plausible justification for the existing estimates. Operate as a bayesian reasoner.

## Other considerations

1. **Cite sources with links** — If you rely on some external source, cite it with a link, using the same link formatting that is used throughout these files.
2. **Every assumption should either be straightforward or have some justification** - If an assumption is not already supported by a high quality link (or several), there should be at least some effort in the details section to justify it. It can be brief and acknowledge lots of uncertainty.
3. **Follow the format of the other estimation files** - If you're adding a new file, look to other similar files and be consistent with their format.
4. **Use standalone assumption pages only when they earn it** - Give an assumption its own page in the `content/assumptions/` directory only when it is reused across pages, is calculation-critical, or would otherwise dominate its parent page. If it can be stated and briefly justified inline, keep it inline. When a page is warranted, these can chain arbitrarily — assumption files can have their own assumptions and refer to other assumption files.
5. **Don't use "I" in these estimates** - If you must, say "we" rather than "I". These estimates are being maintained by many people, not just you.
6. **Don't round numbers up/down unless you have a good reason to** - If you get some number from your calculations and it has too many significant digits, you can round to a reasonable number of significant digits, but don't round up or down unless you have a really strong justification.
7. **Use "# Internal Notes" if it seems helpful** - Each of these files has an "# Internal Notes" section that can be used by editors of the file to discuss relevant considerations which won't be rendered to the user. If there are things you want to put here that may be useful to future editors, do so.
8. **Typeset math as KaTeX, never in backtick code spans** - Calculations and equations go in `$...$` or `$$...$$` (full conventions in the "Math rendering" section of `content/CLAUDE.md`). Two hard rules: escape literal dollar signs as `\$` in prose, and any equation containing a literal `\$` must use `$$...$$` delimiters — inside single-`$` spans the escape is not processed, so the dollar closes the span early and the line renders as broken fragments (mid-line `$$...$$` still renders inline). Code spans are only for literal identifiers like YAML field names; backslash escapes don't work inside them either.
9. **Every published range is an 80% subjective credence interval** - Whenever you state an uncertainty range (a cost-per-QALY range, a parameter range, a p(doom) range, etc.), it means we are ~80% confident the true value lies inside it, with ~10% probability it is lower and ~10% higher. This is a subjective Bayesian credence, so rougher estimates simply get **wider** intervals — there is no separate "sensitivity range" or "confidence interval" concept. Call it a **"plausible range"** everywhere, and at the FIRST mention on a page write the `{{PLAUSIBLE_RANGE}}` token (or `{{PLAUSIBLE_RANGE_CAP}}` to open a sentence or bold label) so it renders the definition tooltip; one token per page, later mentions plain. On cost-per-life category pages the token goes in the section heading — `## Point estimates and {{PLAUSIBLE_RANGES}}` (plural variant) — so it labels the parenthetical ranges shown there. If you compute bounds via a parameter sweep that is narrower than your true 80% interval, widen the stated range to your best-judgment 80% interval rather than presenting the sweep as the range. When editing an existing page, check that the header range matches the true interval argued in the Details — some pages show the narrower sweep in the header while widening it only lower down, and it is the header that should be aligned to the wider interval. When you widen a sweep to reach the published interval, name what the extra width reflects — a parameter the sweep held fixed, or model uncertainty — rather than attributing it to a vague, unquantified adjustment. (Full convention in `content/CLAUDE.md`.)
10. **Put model attribution in Internal Notes only** - Do not add or restore public italic lines crediting the LLM/model that drafted an analysis. If attribution is useful, keep it under the file's `# Internal Notes` section so it is available to editors but stripped from the published page.
11. **Scope before parameters** - Where a page says what kinds of charities or interventions it models (a "What kinds of charities are we modeling?" section or equivalent), put it right after "Description of effect" and before the point estimates and assumptions — the reader should know what is being modeled before seeing the numbers. If its inclusion/exclusion lists run long, collapse them into a `:::details` block while keeping a one-line summary of what is modeled visible.
12. **A parameter sweep is an audit, not the published range** - A sweep (inputs pushed to their edges) is optional — include one only when it genuinely clarifies the estimate, not by default. When you do, present it as a check on the 80% range, not the range itself: put the arithmetic in `:::details`, keep the range in the main argument, and say why the published range is narrower (roughly independent inputs), wider, or about equal (structural/worldview uncertainty dominates). Avoid unexplained jargon — "corner" is fine only if defined on the spot as the all-favorable or all-unfavorable combination of inputs.
